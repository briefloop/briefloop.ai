The real risk

The most dangerous thing about AI reports usually isn't hallucination — it's these “small” errors

They read naturally and sound confident, yet get shipped as final conclusions.

01

A number flips between drafts

The source said “down 1.5%.” By draft five, the body reads “up 2.5%.” No one meant to — but the conclusion is now reversed.

02

The AI invents a reason

The data only shows margins rising; the AI adds “because logistics costs fell” — the material never mentions logistics.

03

Old data written as current

A prior-quarter share figure is cited, but the body says “current market share” — the time range quietly disappears.

Why not just a prompt?

Not a longer prompt — delivery rules outside the AI

Prompts and Skills tell an agent what it should do. BriefLoop records what actually happened, then decides which revision is eligible for delivery.

The essential difference is not another review agent. It is that the writing agent cannot unilaterally declare its own draft ready to deliver.

Writes

ChatGPT + a long prompt

Like bringing in a smart analyst for one assignment.

  • Reads material
  • Summarizes, analyzes, and drafts
  • Revises from feedback
  • Attempts to follow formatting and rules

The rules mostly live in the current conversation. Which draft is final, whether an issue was resolved, and whether a number changed during editing still depend on the model's own explanation.

Best forbrainstorming, one-off drafts, and low-risk internal material.

Runs

Agent runtime + Skill.md

Like giving the analyst tools, a task list, and a standard operating procedure.

  • Calls search, file, and code tools
  • Executes multi-step tasks
  • Reuses Skills and delegates work
  • Retries after failures

Without an independent state and authority layer, the same agent may write, judge itself complete, alter earlier files, and announce a final report. The performer can still be its own judge.

Best forautomated research, information gathering, and internal workflows reviewed manually at the end.

Governs delivery

BriefLoop

It does not replace either of the above. It wraps around them with a record and authority layer:

  • Which material facts are registered
  • Which artifact revision is on record
  • What checks found and whether issues were repaired
  • Which revision passed pre-delivery checks and who approved it
  • Where to resume after an interrupted run

These states live outside the model's reply, in agent-external programs and records.

Best forweeklies, monthlies, executive briefs, IR material, research reports, and work that is repeated, reviewed, or accountable.

ChatGPT thinks and writes. A Skill tells it how. A runtime lets it run. BriefLoop keeps the books, intercepts issues, and controls delivery.

An honest boundary

An agent runtime can certainly build persistent state, frozen artifacts, non-bypassable gates, human approval, audit logs, and recovery by itself. Once it has done so, however, it has effectively rebuilt BriefLoop's core. BriefLoop's value is not that no one else can do this; it is a unified, tested, reusable workflow for the governance layer that teams otherwise assemble from prompts, scripts, and conventions.

A Skill says “please do this.” BriefLoop says “you cannot pass until it is done.”

How it works

Four steps to turn an AI draft into something you can ship

1

Hand your material to the AI

Local files, reports, tables, or registered web material.

2

The AI drafts the report

Use Claude Code, WorkBuddy, or another supported agent.

3

BriefLoop checks it against sources and rules

Whether numbers have sources, whether material is stale, whether important new conclusions appeared.

4

Issues go back for repair; a human confirms before delivery

Unhandled issues never count as final. A human always releases, and it's on the record.

View technical detail

Behind these four steps, each key fact lands in a key-fact ledger (claim_ledger.json) and is frozen; check results, audit findings, and the release action are written to an event log (event_log.jsonl); facts are hashed at freeze time so they can't be quietly changed during editing. These are evidence, not something you read daily — full mechanism on How it works.

Scenarios

Whatever you ship each week, BriefLoop guards that one

What do you ship each week? What does BriefLoop keep for you? What does it most often block?

Industry & competitor weekly

You ship: weekly industry moves and competitor tracking.

It blocks: unverified data and claims mixed in over edit rounds; core numbers are frozen before polishing.

Management monthly

You ship: a monthly operating & decision brief for leadership.

It blocks: “expected” written as “done,” and over-absolute conclusions without attribution.

Document evidence review

You ship: source & compliance review conclusions on AI drafts.

It blocks: stale or unsupported statements are flagged and blocked; review only the exceptions.

Boundary

It helps you find out — it doesn't make the call for you

BriefLoop won't automatically prove a sentence is absolutely true, and it won't replace legal, compliance, or management judgment. What it does is put sources, conflicts, stale material, and open issues in front of you — and keep those from quietly becoming the final draft.

For example: it won't prove “demand definitely isn't recovering”; it only points out that the current material is insufficient to support that conclusion.
